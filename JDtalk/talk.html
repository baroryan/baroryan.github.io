Abstract

The largest, most destructive earthquakes occur at subduction zones, where one tectonic plate sinks beneath another. To mitigate hazards in these regions, scientists routinely measure surface displacement to identify locked areas of the plate interface (megathrust) that are likely to produce megathrust earthquakes. However, these decades-long geodetic observations are limited by their short timespan, capturing only a fraction of the thousands of years over which earthquake cycles persist, raising questions about the reliability of geodetic hazard assessments.

Landscapes, on the other hand, record deformation across many millennia and earthquake cycles, offering a complementary and unique record of tectonic activity. In this talk, I will present two novel frameworks that bridge this gap: the first reveals how earthquake cycles imprint irreversible strain onto topography , while the second translates landscape into quantitative patterns of tectonic forcing. Together, these advances provide a critical foundation for inferring earthquake hazards directly from subduction landscapes worldwide.
